The Problem With Most Advisory

And why systems thinking changes everything

Marketing consultants optimise campaigns without understanding margins. Finance people cut costs without understanding customer value. Technology consultants build systems nobody uses. Each brings expertise in their domain while remaining blind to the others.

The businesses that break through their revenue ceilings do something different. They build infrastructure that connects these disciplines — where marketing spend is informed by unit economics, where automation amplifies human capability rather than replacing it, and where measurement reveals truth rather than vanity.

"The best solutions usually come from connecting dots that specialists never see. Real problems don't respect neat categories."

— Quentin Hunter

This perspective did not come from a book or a course. It emerged from three decades of working across all three disciplines — marketing, finance, and technology — in contexts ranging from global corporations to owner-managed businesses trying to break through their first million.

The patterns repeat with remarkable consistency. The tactics change. The platforms change. The technology changes. But the fundamentals of building a business that scales without its founder? Those have remained constant since the beginning.

What changes is the sophistication of the tools available. Today, AI agents can handle work that once required hiring entire teams. Automation can scale operations without proportional increases in headcount. But these tools only compound growth when they're built on solid strategic foundations.

Without those foundations, technology becomes expensive experimentation. With them, it becomes a genuine multiplier.
